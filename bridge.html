FAQs

Bridge construction involves several challenges, including geotechnical constraints, wind and seismic resistance, foundation stability, and traffic management during execution. In long-span bridges, factors like material selection, aerodynamics, and load distribution are also critical for ensuring durability and safety.

The timeline for tunnel construction varies based on the project complexity. Short urban tunnels can take 6 months to 2 years, while longer highway or railway tunnels can take 3 to 7 years, depending on the geological conditions, construction methods (TBM or drill-and-blast), and safety measures involved.

Tunnel safety includes fire-resistant materials, ventilation systems, emergency exits, drainage systems, and real-time monitoring to ensure structural integrity. Additional measures like automated lighting, air quality monitoring, and IoT-based tracking systems improve operational safety.

Cable-stayed bridges use straight cables connected directly to the towers, distributing load efficiently across the structure. Suspension bridges, on the other hand, use main cables suspended between towers, with vertical cables supporting the deck. Cable-stayed bridges are preferred for shorter spans, while suspension bridges are ideal for long spans over water bodies.

The most common tunnel construction methods include TBM (Tunnel Boring Machine) excavation, drill-and-blast, cut-and-cover, and immersed tube tunneling. Each method is chosen based on the soil type, tunnel length, and environmental conditions.

Geotechnical analysis helps assess soil stability, rock formations, and groundwater conditions, ensuring the foundation of bridges and tunnels can withstand long-term stress and environmental factors. It is crucial for preventing structural failures and optimizing construction techniques.
